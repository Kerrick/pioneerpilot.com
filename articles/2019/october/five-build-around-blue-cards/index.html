Five Build-Around Blue Cards

Published October 27, 2019 by Mrs. Mulligan

Pioneer should be a sweet spot for brewing sweet decks around powerful or unique cards. Because it’s smaller than Modern your brews don’t have to compete with incredibly streamlined and brutal decks. But because it’s larger than standard, unique strategies tend to have more support cards available to make things run smoothly. In this article we look at five build-around blue cards that can inspire your next Pioneer brew.

Number 5: Curious Obsession

Curious Obsession was an impactful card for much of its life in standard, with decks featuring the enchantment becoming streamlined enough to win the first-ever Mythic Championship. It has also seen some fringe play in Modern.

The most obvious way to build around Curious Obsession is to take a Delver-like approach: pack the deck full of cheap, evasive creatures; add a healthy amount of countermagic to protect them and disrupt your opponent; and keep the cards flowing. This is the strategy that both Standard Mono-Blue Tempo decks took and Modern Spirits deck took. Another great approach would be to pick your favorite hexproof creatures, find the best auras in the format, and brew up a Pioneer version of Modern Hexproof (also known as Bogles). However, two other strategies that never overlapped with Curious Obsession might be a fantastic start to your next brew: Heroic and Mono-Blue Devotion from Theros-era Standard.

Heroic decks were almost a hybrid of Delver and Bogles, playing a “protect-the-queen” strategy to establish a threat and kill quickly. Unlike Delver decks they didn't use much of their countermagic to disrupt their opponent, saving it to protect their threats instead. And unlike Bogles, Heroic decks didn’t rely on threats with Hexproof. On the other hand, Mono-Blue Devotion decks flooded the field with a bunch of efficient blue creatures, playing an aggressive strategy. Curious Obsession doesn’t just provide a power and toughness bump plus card draw—it provides a blue pip, too.

This deck is a pretty simple upgrade of previous Standard Heroic decks. It reaches forward in time to upgrade some of the targeting spells like Dive Down and Sheltering Light, but the entire creature package was in standard at the same time. The manabase I’ve sketched out here is a metagame call: if the meta is full of aggressive decks, we probably ought to avoid losing too much life to our lands. If the metagame has more control and midrange decks, though, we might be able to get away with swapping out some copies of Glacial Fortress and Port Town for Mana Confluence, to lower the risk of lands entering tapped in early turns. That’s a big concern for this deck, as it wants to use its mana extremely efficiently in the first four turns of the game. Finally, Pioneer has some pretty great sideboard options in blue & white for playing “protect the queen” both against targeted removal and sweepers. Throw in a bit of removal against fast aggro and Baby Teferi to help against control, and you’ve got a pretty great start.

Number 4: Master of Waves

Master of Waves was instantly recognizable as a powerful card. When it was in standard it immediately became the headliner for Mono-Blue Devotion, a top tier deck that reigned for nearly two years.

Mono-Blue Devotion was an aggressive deck that played efficient creatures on curve, making sure to select threats with a high devotion to blue (a large number of U symbols in their mana cost). In order to break parity once the opponent caught up, it played Master of Waves, Thassa, or her bident to get extra power out of the cards already on the field. Master of waves was often the finisher for the deck, coming down and making lots of little elementals to clog up the board and swing in wide for a final kill.

It was also an instant addition to Modern Merfolk adding to the curve of the Tempo deck, but filling out the deck nicely by giving it a powerful finisher. Modern Merfolk plays lots of little one and two drop merfolk and merfolk “lords” which give other merfolk +1/+1. Since the decks plans to have so many UU creatures on the field, MoW was an obvious addition whenever there was time to play a four drop in modern. Master of Waves comes and goes in the Merfolk decklist based on the metagame. The more combo decks there are the less likely you are to see MoW, but he is a very good addition to the deck if there are a lot of burn or midrange decks in the meta.

Unfortunately Pioneer does not have all of the merfolk lords or Spreading Seas to help get damage across the battlefield, so I think a devotion strategy while still taking some flexibility from the modern tempo deck is the best way to build around Master of Waves in Pioneer.

This list is built with efficient mono-blue creatures but I upgraded them to include cards that weren’t available at the time of Mono Blue Devotion in standard. Mausoleum wanderer is a strong upgrade for Judge’s Familiar and Unsubstantiate is an upgrade for Cyclonic Rift. Tempest Djinn is a very good card on its own and made an appearance in standard, but with it costing UUU it fits really nicely into a Blue devotion shell. Faerie Miscreant and Bident of Thassa make an excellent combo, and while Shorecrasher Elemental was not the strongest card on its own, providing three blue pips brings it over the edge into a card we are happy to play in this deck. Master of Waves pulls it all together giving it the final splash it needs to flood the board with creatures and finish off the game. The sideboard will change as the metagame settles out, but I know that this deck is likely to be weak against combo and control so the included sideboard is built to combat those strategies.

Number 3: Narcomoeba

Narcomoeba has always been a powerful enabler of extremely unfair graveyard-based decks, usually built around the Dredge mechanic—even in 10th Edition Standard. In Legacy and Vintage its most powerful trait is that it’s a free creature used to cast flashback spells like Dread Return. In Modern, the blue illusion is more often used to trigger Prized Amalgam’s return from the graveyard. However, in formats without Dredge it requires a bit more effort to get value out of Narcomoeba. Just this month Kanister used a “Mono-Blue” Phoenix deck with Drowned Secrets to mill himself all the way to a Top 4 finish at Fandom Legends.

Given that Pioneer is Dredge-free (for now?), we‘ll have to make do with more awkward ways to self-mill: Drowned Secrets can form a great base for this, and naturally pushes us towards playing cards like Tome Scour and Venture Deeper to spill our deck out into our graveyard.

Once we’re committed to playing low-cost spells like Tome Scour and Venture Deeper to mill ourself with Drowned Secrets, two things become apparent: you’re likely to be able to trigger Arclight Phoenix with this deck; and I really wish Thought Scour was in Pioneer. The first revelation means we’ll want the closest thing to Faithless Looting we can get in the format: Izzet Charm. It also means Prized Amalgam can be a slam-dunk, because it’ll be triggered both by Narcomoeba and Phoenix. The second problem was a bit tougher.

Goldfishing with initial drafts of the deck showed just how painful it can be to cast non-cantripping self-mill cards—I was constantly running out of cards in my hand before getting through enough of my deck. Enter: Treasure Cruise. When playing any self-mill deck you often end up keeping track of “important” and “uninmportant” piles in your graveyard. With this build the unimportant pile grows so fast that Cruise really does feel like Ancestral Recall a lot of the time. The addition of a playset of the golden boat really tied the deck together.

The final addition of Creeping Chill, as is popular in so many self-mill decks, also brought one more idea into clear focus: sometimes you draw the cards you’d rather mill. C’est la vie. Pioneer’s mana is good enough that an aggressive deck like ours can play three colors without worrying too much. Mana Confluence and shock lands hurt, but they’re better than a Creeping Chill rotting in your hand.

The sideboard of this deck is an interesting one, but I’ll leave that mostly to you as an exercise. Here are some basic ideas: a transformative Thing in the Ice sideboard plan; a tricky swap to Young Pyromancer once the opponent brings out their removal; or shoring up certain matchups with anti-hate cards and counterspells. What kind of sideboard would you build?
